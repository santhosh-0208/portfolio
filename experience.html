Experience

Force Control Sensor Design Robotics Deployment

Robotics Engineer

Svaya Robotics | 2023 - Present

Developing sensing, control, and autonomy technologies for six- and seven-axis collaborative robots, taking systems from R&D and validation through field deployment.

My work spans the full robotics stack, including force sensing, control systems, motion planning, calibration, safety validation, and deployment support.

What I've Built

Joint & Force-Torque Sensors

Designed custom Joint Torque Sensors and six-axis force-torque sensors across multiple load capacities, balancing structural compliance, accuracy, and weight. Collaborating with Design, FEA, embedded and motion planning teams was a crucial part of the development process. These sensors provide the tactile feedback necessary for robots to perform delicate manipulation tasks reliably.

Force-Control Algorithms

Developed impedance control, free-drive, and hybrid force-position control for contact-rich industrial tasks. These control strategies enable robots to collaborate safely with humans and adapt to uncertain environments.

Safety & Collision Systems

Built real-time collision detection and safety validation systems, including custom impact-force measurement rigs for collaborative robot benchmarking. Safety is paramount in human-robot collaboration, and these systems ensure reliable protection for workers.

Calibration & Validation Pipelines

Developed testing and validation pipelines to improve assembly reliability and deployment readiness. Automated validation catches issues early and ensures consistent quality across production units.

Customer Deployment & Root-Cause Analysis

Supported customer deployments and translated field observations into product and R&D improvements. Real-world feedback is invaluable for understanding how systems behave outside controlled laboratory conditions.

Impact & Learning

Working on production robotics systems has taught me the importance of bridging the gap between elegant theory and practical deployment. The challenges of real-world robotics are fundamentally different from academic problems— every design decision must account for cost, reliability, manufacturability, and field serviceability.

This experience reinforced a core principle: good robotics engineering is not about building the most sophisticated system, but about building the right system for the right application, with the right balance of capability and robustness.
