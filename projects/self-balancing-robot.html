Self Balancing Robot

Control Systems Embedded Systems Reinforcement Learning
View on GitHub

Project Overview

Inverted pendulums are always fascinating. They are simple enough to understand on paper, but difficult enough to expose every weakness in your model, controller, hardware, and assumptions. A lot can change depending on the system dynamics, type of motors you use, and even the sampling rate of your control loop. This project began as an attempt to build a two-wheeled self-balancing robot from scratch and use it as a platform to learn controls, embedded systems, and eventually reinforcement learning. Check out the advanced version of this I'm currently working on, the one with 8 degrees of freedom and whole-body control: 8-DOF Balancing Robot.

I designed and assembled the hardware, integrated the sensors and motor drivers, and developed the software needed to estimate the robot's state in real time. To understand the system properly, I derived its dynamics and performed frequency response analysis to identify the plant characteristics before designing a controller.

Hardware Development

The physical robot consists of:

  • Two DC motors with wheel encoders for velocity feedback
  • 9-axis IMU for attitude and angular velocity measurement
  • Microcontroller running the real-time control loop
  • Motor drivers for bidirectional motor control
  • Mechanical frame for housing all components

Control Systems

The first balancing attempts were far less graceful than the final result. Small errors in sensor calibration, delays in the control loop, and noise in the measurements would quickly cause the robot to oscillate or fall over.

Solving these issues taught me more about practical control systems than any textbook could:

  • Sensor Calibration - Ensuring IMU measurements are accurate and bias-corrected
  • State Estimation - Using complementary filters to fuse accelerometer and gyro data
  • Control Loop Timing - Meeting strict timing requirements for stability
  • Noise Management - Filtering noisy sensor data without introducing excessive lag

Once a stable PID controller was working reliably, the robot could balance indefinitely and respond to disturbances smoothly.

Classical Control vs. Learning-Based Approaches

Once the PID controller was stable, I became curious about how modern learning-based methods would compare against classical control. I trained a reinforcement learning policy and deployed it directly onto the physical hardware, using IMU measurements as inputs and motor commands as outputs.

What made the project interesting was not determining whether PID or reinforcement learning was "better," but understanding the strengths and limitations of each approach:

  • Classical Control - Provided predictability, stability guarantees, and zero-shot transfer
  • Learning-Based - Offered flexibility, learned directly from physical data, but required training time and raised robustness questions

Key Insights

This project became a personal playground for exploring the relationship between physics, control theory, embedded systems, and machine learning. It remains one of the projects that most influenced how I think about robotics today.

The most important lessons:

  • Theory meets reality in subtle and often unexpected ways
  • Hardware constraints and timing matter more than elegant mathematics
  • Different tools excel in different domains—sometimes the best solution uses both
  • Building physical systems teaches lessons that simulations often miss
